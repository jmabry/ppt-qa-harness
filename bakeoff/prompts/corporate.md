**Analyst notes on the multi-year arc:** The EPS story looks completely flat in isolation — $10.61 in 2022, $10.05 in 2023, $10.61 in 2024, $10.62 in 2025 — and that's why some investors have dismissed UAL as a value trap since the post-COVID recovery. But that framing misses the point entirely. Revenue is up 31% since 2022 on a 33% ASM increase, so the company is flying significantly more seats and carrying significantly more passengers. The reason earnings haven't moved is not operational underperformance — it's a deliberate, front-loaded absorption of the largest labor cost step-up in airline history. The pilot contract ratified in mid-2023 added $10B in cumulative obligation with 34–40% wage increases, retroactive in part, that hit the P&L over 2023 and 2024. The flight attendant tentative agreement done in early 2025 layered on another 20–28% wage increase for the second-largest cost group. These contracts were necessary — the airline was flying with pre-pandemic labor economics in a post-pandemic labor market — but they created a multi-year earnings plateau that is now largely behind the company. Meanwhile, on the other side of the ledger, fleet efficiency is compounding: every new 737 MAX and A321neo that replaces a legacy narrowbody burns ~20% less fuel per seat and requires fewer crew per seat served (because the new planes are larger). The 2026–2027 setup is that all the known labor headwinds are already priced into guidance, while the aircraft efficiency tailwinds keep accumulating with every delivery. The $12–14 EPS guidance for 2026 would be the first time UAL has materially exceeded its 2019 peak of $12.05 — which is the signal management believes the multi-year investment cycle is finally producing returns rather than just absorbing costs.

**Analyst read on unit economics:** The TRASM decline of –2.5% in FY2025 vs. FY2024 is the single most important data point to address head-on in the deck, because it's what bears use to argue against the margin expansion thesis. The important nuance is that this is not a demand problem — passengers hit a company record of 181M, load factors were solid at 82.2%, and booking trends exiting the year were the strongest in UAL's history. The problem was pure industry capacity discipline (or lack of it): the major carriers, United included, added seats in 2025 at a pace that outran the willingness of domestic leisure and connecting passengers to pay higher fares. When supply grows faster than demand at the margin, PRASM declines even if total revenue is up — which is exactly what happened. UAL's response was to pull 4 percentage points of scheduled domestic capacity mid-year and retire 21 aircraft ahead of plan, which is management signaling that they're willing to sacrifice near-term growth to protect yield. That's a meaningful data point for the forward thesis. On the cost side, the CASM-ex increase of +0.5% sounds minor but represents a significant tug-of-war: fuel was a genuine tailwind (saving roughly $360M on lower price per gallon) while labor consumed $969M more year-over-year. The net result is a cost structure that grew slightly faster than revenue, compressing margins — but the composition matters. Fuel tailwinds could reverse but new aircraft efficiency partially insulates; labor headwinds are known and peaking. The structural argument is: TRASM inflects up as industry capacity normalizes in 2026, CASM-ex stabilizes as aircraft deliveries bring efficiency, and the gap between the two lines widens into margin expansion. That is the entire 2026 thesis in one sentence.

Fuel sensitivity is worth flagging explicitly and prominently for institutional readers: UAL carries no financial fuel hedges by policy. Every $0.10/gallon move in jet fuel translates to roughly $466M in annual P&L impact at 2025 consumption volumes of 4.663B gallons. At $2.44/gal in 2025, a move back toward the 2023 level of $3.01 would be a ~$2.7B headwind — material against a ~$4.6B adjusted pre-tax income base.

**Analyst notes on cost composition:** The deck should not treat OpEx growth as a monolith — the composition tells very different stories about durability and future trajectory. Three lines deserve flag-and-explain treatment.

First, salaries. Up $2.9B in just two years — from $14.8B in 2023 to $17.6B in 2025 — this is the dominant story in the cost structure and will remain so through 2027 when the pilot contract expires. Labor is now 32.5% of total operating expense. The right frame for investors is not "this is bad" but "this is known, bounded, and mostly behind us." The pilot contract signed mid-2023 carries the heaviest wage-rate increases in the early years and steps down in incremental burden by 2026–2027. The FA deal done in 2025 adds to the base but removes a multi-year labor relations overhang. And critically, the gauge-up strategy — replacing small regional jets with large mainline aircraft — means the labor cost per seat served is rising less than the labor cost per employee, because each new mainline departure serves 150+ passengers vs. 50 on the planes it replaces.

Second, landing fees and rent, up 12%. This sounds alarming but is almost entirely self-inflicted in a good way: UAL's own $2.7B EWR Terminal A investment (opened 2023), the ongoing ORD Terminal 2 satellite renovation, and DEN gate expansions are creating higher rent obligations as the company moves into its own brand-new facilities. The correlation between this cost line and NPS improvement is not coincidental. Investors should understand this as deferred premium product spending showing up in the OpEx line rather than in CapEx.

Third, distribution, down 5.5%. This is the hidden tailwind in the cost structure. As UAL shifts booking mix away from global distribution systems (GDS, which charge per-ticket fees) toward direct channels — UAL.com, the mobile app, which now handles 85% of check-ins — the distribution cost per ticket falls. This trend has legs because the app engagement metrics are accelerating, not plateauing, and direct bookings allow UAL to capture more ancillary revenue and better loyalty data in the same transaction.

**Analyst read on regional mix:** The story of 2025 UAL is fundamentally a two-speed airline operating within a single P&L, and the regional breakdown is where that shows up most clearly. Domestic fares were soft all year — PRASM negative in every single quarter, worst at –7.0% in Q2 — while international, particularly Atlantic and Pacific, was holding or gaining yield. The implication for 2026 is that international momentum is durable (it's structural, not cyclical) while domestic is the variable that management believes it can fix through industry capacity discipline. If they're right, the earnings leverage is substantial: domestic is still 54%+ of passenger revenue, so even modest PRASM improvement on that base compounds quickly.

The MEIA line (Middle East / India / Africa) deserves a dedicated callout as an emerging growth vector that most models haven't fully priced. The +58.7% Q4 revenue growth with +56% capacity deployment tells you it's all new routes being ramped — not yield improvement on existing flying. EWR–Dubai, EWR–Delhi, and SFO–Bangalore are all relatively new additions with limited year-over-year base. India in particular is a structural opportunity: it's the world's most populous country, with a rapidly growing outbound travel market (both business and diaspora), and U.S. carriers have historically underserved it. UAL is arguably the most aggressive U.S. airline in India and the Gulf, and the A321XLR in 2026 gives them more tool sets to expand beyond gateway hubs.

The Pacific story is a recovery-to-normal framing: Japan reopened fully, capacity came back, and UAL pushed beyond to add Bangkok (only U.S. carrier), Adelaide (only U.S. carrier), and Ho Chi Minh City (only U.S. carrier). These "only U.S. carrier" monopoly routes are meaningful — they carry premium business passengers who have no alternative, which supports yield.

Latin America is the one region with genuine softness. PRASM –13.5% in Q3 and –7.6% in Q4 reflects Brazilian carrier competition on key corridors and some LatAm macro headwinds. The good news is Latin is only ~10% of passenger revenue, so it's a manageable drag rather than a thesis-breaker.

**Analyst notes on premium + loyalty:** This is the quality-of-earnings story, and it's the part of the UAL thesis that gets the least credit in bear-case analyses. Premium cabin revenue grew +11% in 2025 while basic economy grew only +5% — the mix is shifting, deliberately and measurably, toward higher-yield passengers who are less likely to trade down when the economy softens. This is not a coincidence; it's the direct output of the Signature Interior rollout (119 aircraft, 68% of narrowbody fleet), the gauge-up program (more premium rows per departure as regional jets exit), and the focused marketing of Economy Plus and Polaris to frequent travelers who buy on loyalty and schedule, not on price. The structural implication is that UAL's revenue base is becoming less commodity-like with each passing quarter even if headline TRASM looks flat.

On loyalty specifically: the program has 130M members and is adding more than 1M new Chase co-brand cardholders per year for the third consecutive year. That's a customer acquisition rate that most consumer financial products would envy, and it's happening at effectively zero incremental cost to UAL because Chase is absorbing the acquisition economics. The card spend growing at +12% YoY while the base is already large suggests the existing member base is becoming more engaged — which feeds miles accrual, which feeds redemption, which feeds flights. It's a compounding flywheel. The CEO's public target of doubling MileagePlus profits by 2030 is ambitious but grounded in the Chase deal dynamics: the current contract runs to 2029, and as the partnership approaches renewal, UAL's negotiating leverage depends on demonstrating scale growth — which they're clearly doing.

The valuation anchor here that every analyst should include: in 2020, United used MileagePlus as collateral for a $6.8B financing package, at which point bankers and S&P valued the program at approximately $22B. UAL has since repaid all of that financing and freed the collateral. The program has grown since 2020 in members, card additions, and annual cash generation. Yet UAL's total market cap today is approximately $28B. The math implies investors are ascribing minimal standalone value to MileagePlus above what's embedded in the airline — and if you separate the two the way Delta/AmEx is valued independently in investor models, there's a significant re-rating argument.

**Key debt action (major event):** In Q3 2025 UAL prepaid the remaining $1.5B of MileagePlus secured bonds, fully retiring $6.8B in MileagePlus-backed financing by July 2025. This was the collateralized debt package taken on during COVID to survive the shutdown. Paying it off entirely frees the MileagePlus asset, eliminates collateral restrictions, and signals management confidence in the balance sheet recovery. Interest expense dropped 15.7% YoY ($1.629B → $1.373B) — that flow-through goes straight to EPS.

**Adjusted net debt (Q1 2025):** $18.847B vs. $22.462B in Q1 2024. Deleveraging pace is roughly $3–4B per year.

**Investment-grade path and why it matters:** At the current deleveraging trajectory — roughly $3–4B of net debt reduction per year — UAL reaches below 2.0× leverage by year-end 2026 as guided. S&P's published criteria for investment-grade airlines historically sit in the 2.0–2.5× adjusted debt/EBITDA range with demonstrated earnings stability. The company is currently at 2.2× and falling. Delta already carries BBB-. An investment-grade upgrade for UAL would have three distinct financial consequences worth spelling out for investors. First, the cost of new aircraft financing drops materially — airlines finance most of their fleet; even 50–75 bps of lower borrowing cost on $25B of debt is $125–190M of annual interest savings that flow directly to EPS. Second, IG mandates open a materially larger investor universe — many institutional bond and equity funds have IG-only mandates, and an upgrade would bring incremental demand into the stock from funds that literally cannot hold it today. Third, and perhaps most important psychologically, IG status completes the narrative arc from COVID bankruptcy risk to investment-quality enterprise, which tends to produce a multiple re-rating in how the market values the earnings stream. The question for active investors is not whether the upgrade happens — the trajectory makes it highly probable — but whether to own the stock before the rating action (capturing the re-rating) or after. Most who follow the sector are betting before.

### STARLINK CONNECTIVITY ROLLOUT

- May 2025: First E175 regional with Starlink enters service
- October 15, 2025: First mainline commercial Starlink flight (EWR)
- February 2026: 300+ mainline aircraft equipped
- End of 2026: 800+ total aircraft (target ~25% of departures in early phase; growing to full fleet)
- End of 2027: Full fleet completion (all aircraft)
- Install pace: 50+ aircraft per month
- Offered free to all MileagePlus members

The connectivity story matters for reasons well beyond passenger experience, and investors tend to underweight it because the revenue numbers are still small. The first-order effect is NPS: Signature Interior aircraft — which now include seatback screens and larger bins — score 10 points higher on NPS than the planes they replace, and Starlink further separates UAL's inflight product from American (no free Wi-Fi) and partially from Delta (Viasat system, not Starlink). When you're flying a business traveler who does this route 40 times a year, 10 NPS points translates into retention and card spend.

The second-order effect is Kinective Media, which is where institutional investors should be paying close attention. UAL launched this programmatic advertising network in 2024 on the back of three assets no other airline possesses at the same scale simultaneously: 227,000+ seatback screens across a captive audience, 108M unique annual flyers with known demographics, and 130M MileagePlus members with deep first-party transaction data — miles earned, redemptions, credit card spending, partner hotel and car bookings. This is a better audience profile than most digital advertising platforms can offer, because the data is behavioral and purchase-intent-based, not just demographic. The CFO explicitly said on the earnings call that Kinective "will really start to accelerate in '26 and beyond" — this language was notably specific. Media margins are dramatically higher than seat revenue margins. Even a few hundred million dollars of high-margin ad revenue moving into the model changes the EBITDA profile in a way that seat revenue cannot. Delta has SkyMiles advertising and some screen inventory, but nothing at this level of integration between connectivity, data, and programmatic capability. If UAL executes on Kinective, it's a new business that the market is currently valuing at approximately zero.

SAF at 13.6M gallons in 2024 vs. 4.2B+ total fuel consumed — less than 0.35%. The trajectory is genuinely impressive (roughly doubling year-over-year since 2021) but the gap between where SAF is and where it needs to be to matter for the 2050 target is enormous. You'd need something like 4B gallons of SAF annually to run a meaningful portion of the fleet — the entire current global SAF supply is a fraction of that. United Airlines Ventures is investing in the supply chain companies (Twelve for CO2-to-SAF conversion, ZeroAvia for hydrogen-electric propulsion, Dimensional Energy, Cemvita, Svante for carbon capture) specifically because management understands that purchasing agreements alone won't solve the supply problem. The $200M+ UAL SAF Fund is as much about shaping the supply curve as it is about near-term procurement. The Eco-Skies Alliance — 50+ corporate partners co-investing in SAF purchases — is a clever structure: it socializes the cost of developing the SAF supply ecosystem across UAL's corporate customers rather than internalizing it entirely.

The honest ESG framing for a sophisticated institutional audience, and the deck should not shy away from it: UAL's absolute emissions are rising with capacity growth, and will continue to rise for the foreseeable future as UAL adds aircraft and flies more people. The 2035 target is intensity-based (CO2e per RTK or ASM), which is achievable with newer, more efficient aircraft. The 2050 net zero target is aspirationally ambitious and dependent on technologies — green hydrogen, next-generation SAF, potential carbon removal — that don't exist at commercial scale today. The appropriate investor read is: UAL is doing as much as any airline in the world on climate, the targets are credible relative to peers, and the Ventures portfolio is a genuine strategic investment rather than greenwashing — but don't model material emissions reductions into the near-term financial case, because SAF is still a rounding error in the fuel budget.

**Analyst note on labor economics:** The pilot contract through 2027 is the known, bounded, and increasingly digested liability in the UAL cost model. Management has been guiding around it for two years, investors have heard the math repeatedly, and the 2026 guidance already prices in the full run-rate impact. What's less appreciated is that the FA deal completion in early 2025 actually removed a significant overhang: the flight attendant contract had been in negotiation for years, creating operational uncertainty and some degree of labor-relations friction. Having all four major labor groups under current agreements simultaneously — pilots through 2027, FAs through approximately the same window, mechanics since 2024, ground workers updated 2024–25 — means UAL enters 2026 with the most stable labor environment since before the pandemic. That has operational value that doesn't show up in the P&L directly but shows up in completion rates, NPS, and customer experience consistency.

The structural offset to higher unit labor costs is the gauge-up arithmetic, and it's worth explaining precisely for the model-builders in the room. When UAL replaces a regional partner operating two 50-seat CRJ departures with a single 150-seat 737 MAX departure on the same route, the pilot cost per departure roughly triples (larger mainline aircraft requires higher-paid mainline pilots), but the pilot cost per seat served drops materially because you have 150 passengers paying instead of 100. The same logic applies to flight attendant staffing ratios, training amortization, and ground handling. The gauge-up strategy is, at its core, a labor productivity strategy disguised as a fleet strategy — and it only compounds further as the CRJ450 regional upgrade brings Signature-class product to the remaining smaller aircraft without the structural cost burden of the old 50-seaters. The critical path risk: this arithmetic only works if Boeing and Airbus keep delivering aircraft on schedule, which is currently the single biggest operational variable outside UAL's direct control.
